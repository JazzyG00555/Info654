Brief History

by Lauren Holmes

During post-WWII USSR, the state attempted to tightly control popular youth culture and shun Western influence. Despite Soviet attempts, Western music, mainly jazz (1940s and 1950s) and rock music (1960-1990s) seeped through the Iron Curtain to create an underground, illegal music evolution and communities, largely in major cities like Leningrad (now St. Petersburg), Moscow and Sverdlovsk (now Yekaterinburg) (Ryback, 1989).

The Soviet Union’s Ministry of Culture quickly realized the state's inability to stem the tide of Western music and eventually attempted to tame rock music’s influence by infusing state approved, Soviet value-drenched rock music beginning in the 1980s with state sanctioned (and censored) music, bands, and songs (Ryback, 1989). The state controled recording label, called Melodiya, had total control over all official music releases until the Societ Union's fall in 1991, for more on Melodiya, click here. Benefits of being a state-controled band, called vocal instrument assemblies (VIA) was access to quality instruments and equipment shipped from the West and financial stability (Ryback, 1989). The underground musicians created DIY (do-it-yourself) instruments out of telephones and furniture, while maintaining full time jobs, many times as janitors or security personnel for more hour flexibility (Ryback, 1989). These two rock communities (official and unofficial) circulated simultaneously during the 1980s, a decade generally regarded as the "golden age" of Russian rock music (Steinholt, 2004).

Russian rock music infused Russian folk and bard elements with jazz and Western rock music, creating a unique sound and adapting to their own experiences and access (Ryback, 1989), to purchase a copy click here. Borrowing from bands such as Queen, Deep Purple, and the Beatles, Russian rock music imitated but tailored their sound, many times for political rebellion against VIAs and the Ministry of Culture (Steinholt, 2004). When rock music was finally infused into official popular culture, as the Soviet Union began to fall, rock music became widely available through VIAs (Steinholt, 2004).

Since the fall of the USSR in 1991, rock music has also dropped off of popular culture. Seen as a rather dead genre, some Russian rock still lingers with bands such as SHARY (ШАРЫ) and others touring around Russia, but rarely in Europe or other continents, for a brief review of Russian rock, click here.

Interesting Facts

  • During the 1950s, during Russia’s Beatlemania, because real music equipment and instruments were unavailable, they would record sound not on vinyl records, but discarded x-ray sheets stolen from dumpsters outside of hospitals. Rumor has it that there were many recordings of Elvis on x-rays of pelvises, for more click here to read about the history of bone recordings in "A Short History of Bone."

Sound recording on used x-rays, from "A Short History of Bone."
  • Similarly, without official equipment, DIY rock enthusiats would tamper with telephones to make microphones, amplifiers and even guitars. Though hardly the best quality, these initiatives allowed for youths to express themselves, even if the intention was not to rebel against the Soviet government, for more on Soviet guitars, click here from "How the electric guitar sidestepped the Cold War to rock the USSR."
  • The Leningrad Rock Club, written about extensively in Yngvar Bordrewich Steinholt's PhD thesis in 2004 for the University of Tromsø, Norway, was a historic music venue in Leningrad that housed many of what would become the famous rock bands in the 1980s, such as Mashina Vremini and Aquarium. For musicians to have this space ushered in the "golden age" of Russian rock music in the 1980s to the downfall of the Soviet Union, permitting the gathering of the Soviet youth (Steinholt, 2004).

Aquarium playing onstage at Leningrad Rock Club, 1985, from "A Haven For Soviet Rock And Roll Is Long Gone But Its Music Still Resonates."
  • Though bands and songs were not eplicitly political, their existance was seen as rebellion against Soviet culture and values of uniformity, short cropped hair and appropriately fitting pants. Lying outside the cultural establishment, both unofficial and official rock clubs would be raided and people arrested for their attendance and unkempt behavior (Steinholt, 2004; Ryback, 1989).

Crowd at Leningrad Rock Club from "A Haven For Soviet Rock And Roll Is Long Gone But Its Music Still Resonates."
  • During the beginning of the Soviet rock scene in the late 1960s, the dance move the Twist was outlawed, particularly because of the Beatles. During dances, young people would be reprimanded for such behavior. In addition, Soviet youth were encouraged to "dance apart" in order to decrease the likelihood of any kind of dancing deemed sexual (Ryback, 1989).
